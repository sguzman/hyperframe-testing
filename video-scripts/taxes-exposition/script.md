# Video Script: The Tax Machine: How America Funds Itself

## Cold open

Every government has a constitution on paper.

But it also has a constitution in cash.

The tax system is where political authority becomes operational. It tells us who the state can reach, what kinds of income it can measure, which activities it rewards, which activities it punishes, and which compromises were too useful to remove.

In the United States, this system is not one machine.

It is several machines stacked on top of each other.

Federal income taxes. Payroll taxes. Corporate taxes. Tariffs. Excise taxes. State sales taxes. Local property taxes. Deductions. Credits. Exclusions. Special rules.

Some of these taxes are visible. Some are almost invisible. Some are paid once a year. Some are withheld before the worker ever sees the money. Some are hidden in prices. Some are buried inside property bills. Some are not taxes in the ordinary sense at all, but spending programs disguised as tax preferences.

This video is a map of that machine.

Not how to file your taxes.

Not how to lower your bill.

But how the American tax system is built.

## 1. The basic architecture

Start with the first distinction.

There is no single American tax system.

There is a federal tax system. There are state tax systems. There are local tax systems. And they do not all work the same way.

The federal government is dominated by income and payroll taxation. It mainly reaches income: wages, salaries, business profits, corporate profits, capital gains, dividends, estates, imports, and certain products or activities.

State and local governments are different. They rely much more heavily on property taxes, sales taxes, and state income taxes. Local governments, especially, are built around property.

So when people say "taxes" in America, they are usually compressing several different systems into one word.

At the federal level, the main machine has two engines: the individual income tax and the payroll tax.

In fiscal year 2025, individual income taxes brought in about $2.684 trillion. Payroll taxes brought in about $1.766 trillion. Together, they accounted for roughly 84 percent of federal receipts.

That is the first major fact.

The federal government is not mainly funded by tariffs. It is not mainly funded by corporate taxes. It is not mainly funded by estate taxes.

It is mainly funded by taxing personal income and payroll.

Corporate income taxes raised $456 billion. Customs duties raised $194 billion. Excise taxes raised $105 billion. Estate and gift taxes raised $31 billion.

Those numbers matter because they show the shape of the state. The federal tax system is not just a list of rates. It is a revenue structure. And that structure reveals where the government can collect money at scale. 

## 2. The individual income tax

The tax most people think about first is the federal individual income tax.

This is the familiar progressive tax with brackets.

For tax year 2026, the ordinary federal individual income tax has seven rates: 10 percent, 12 percent, 22 percent, 24 percent, 32 percent, 35 percent, and 37 percent.

But this is often misunderstood.

A progressive tax bracket does not mean all your income is taxed at your highest rate. It means income is taxed in layers.

The first layer is taxed at one rate. The next layer at another. Then another.

For single filers in 2026, the top 37 percent bracket begins at $640,600. For married couples filing jointly, it begins at $768,700.

Before those rates apply, most taxpayers get a standard deduction. In 2026, the standard deduction is $16,100 for single filers and $32,200 for joint filers.

So the individual income tax is not just a rate schedule. It is a rate schedule after deductions, exclusions, credits, filing statuses, and definitions of income.

That last phrase matters: definitions of income.

Because one of the central questions in tax policy is not only "what rate should apply?"

It is "what counts as taxable income in the first place?"

Wages are treated one way. Long-term capital gains are treated another. Employer health insurance is treated another. Retirement contributions are treated another. Municipal bond interest may be treated another. Imputed rent from owning a home is mostly not taxed at all.

The tax system begins to look less like a simple calculator and more like a classification machine.

It sorts income into categories.

Then it applies different rules to each category.

## 3. Payroll taxes: the automatic engine

The second federal engine is payroll taxation.

This is the part many people feel but do not always analyze.

Payroll taxes fund Social Security and Medicare. They are collected directly from wages. In that sense, they are extremely efficient. They do not wait for April. They do not depend on a person writing a check. They are withheld through the payroll system.

For 2026, the Social Security wage base is $184,500. Workers pay 6.2 percent for Social Security up to that wage base, and employers pay another 6.2 percent. For self-employment income, the combined rate is 12.4 percent.

Medicare is different because it is not capped in the same way.

The important structural point is this: payroll taxes are flatter than the income tax, and they apply heavily to labor income.

This creates a different distributional profile.

The individual income tax is explicitly progressive. Payroll taxes are broad, automatic, and wage-centered. They are politically powerful because they are tied to major social insurance programs. But they also make the federal system less purely progressive than income tax brackets alone might suggest.

This is why tax debates often become confused.

A person may look only at federal income taxes and see one pattern.

Another person may look at payroll taxes and see another.

A complete analysis has to include both.

## 4. Capital income: a different track

Now move from labor income to capital income.

Short-term capital gains are generally taxed like ordinary income. But long-term capital gains and qualified dividends are treated differently.

They use a preferential schedule: 0 percent, 15 percent, and 20 percent, with an additional 3.8 percent net investment income tax for higher-income taxpayers.

This is one of the most important structural separations in the tax code.

The system distinguishes between income earned by working and income earned by owning.

That does not mean capital income is untaxed. It is taxed. But it is often taxed under a different logic.

The policy arguments are familiar: investment, risk, inflation, double taxation, economic growth, lock-in effects.

But the institutional result is simple.

The code creates separate lanes.

Labor income enters one lane.

Long-term capital gains and qualified dividends enter another.

That matters because capital gains are highly concentrated at the top of the income distribution. So even when the tax system is progressive overall, the treatment of capital income has major distributional consequences.

This is also where the tax system becomes visibly political. Not in the shallow sense of party slogans, but in the deeper sense that the code has to decide what kinds of economic activity deserve favorable treatment.

The tax system does not merely collect revenue.

It ranks activities.

## 5. Corporate taxes: not the whole business story

The federal corporate income tax has a 21 percent statutory rate.

That rate was established by the 2017 tax reform, which permanently lowered the federal corporate rate from its previous level.

But the corporate tax is not simply "corporations pay 21 percent."

There are deductions. Credits. Timing rules. International rules. Depreciation rules. Loss rules. Sector-specific provisions. And now, for very large corporations, a 15 percent corporate alternative minimum tax created by the Inflation Reduction Act.

That alternative minimum tax applies to very large corporations based on adjusted financial statement income, generally for firms above a statutory income threshold.

So the corporate tax system has become a layered regime.

There is the ordinary corporate income tax. There is the corporate alternative minimum tax. There are excise taxes like the stock buyback excise tax. And there are many provisions that affect when income is recognized and how investment costs are deducted.

Corporate taxation is also politically strange because corporations are legal taxpayers, but the economic burden may fall on shareholders, workers, consumers, or some combination of them.

That is called tax incidence.

And incidence is one of the most important ideas in taxation.

The person legally responsible for paying a tax is not always the person who ultimately bears the cost.

Tariffs are imposed on imports. But the cost may be passed to consumers.

Payroll taxes are split between employer and employee on paper. But economists often analyze how much of the burden ultimately falls on workers through wages.

Corporate taxes are paid by corporations. But the economic burden may be distributed across capital owners, workers, and consumers.

So the tax code has a legal surface and an economic underside.

A serious analysis has to look at both.

## 6. Tariffs and excise taxes: taxes through prices

Not all federal taxes are built around income.

Some are built around transactions, products, imports, or activities.

Excise taxes are product- or activity-specific. They can apply to things like fuel, tobacco, alcohol, airline tickets, and other targeted categories.

Tariffs are duties collected at the border.

These taxes do not feel like income taxes. They often appear as higher prices, altered supply chains, or changed business costs.

That makes them politically useful and analytically dangerous.

Useful, because they can raise revenue or protect industries without looking like a direct tax bill to households.

Dangerous, because the burden can be hidden inside prices and supply chains.

In 2026, the tariff regime is especially fluid. The report notes a temporary 10 percent ad valorem import surcharge on covered imports beginning February 24, 2026, subject to exclusions and interactions with other tariff authorities.

That is a reminder that tariffs are not just a tax category. They are also trade policy.

A tariff raises revenue, but it also changes incentives. It affects importers, exporters, consumers, domestic producers, and foreign suppliers.

The income tax asks, "How much did you earn?"

A tariff asks, "What crossed the border?"

That is a different kind of state vision.

## 7. The estate tax: small revenue, large symbolism

The estate and gift tax is a relatively small revenue source.

In fiscal year 2025, estate and gift taxes raised about $31 billion. Compared with individual income taxes and payroll taxes, that is small.

But the estate tax is symbolically large because it touches inheritance, wealth transmission, family property, and the intergenerational structure of inequality.

For 2026, the federal estate tax basic exclusion amount is $15 million.

That means the estate tax applies only to a small fraction of estates.

So we should be precise.

The estate tax is not a broad-based middle-class tax. It is not a major federal revenue engine. But it is a major philosophical marker.

It asks whether very large accumulations of wealth should pass between generations without a federal claim at death.

In the tax system, not every provision matters because it raises enormous money.

Some provisions matter because they define the moral boundary of the fiscal state.

## 8. Tax expenditures: spending hidden inside the code

Now we reach one of the most important parts of the machine.

Tax expenditures.

A tax expenditure is government spending through the tax code.

Instead of the government sending a check, it reduces tax liability through an exclusion, deduction, credit, deferral, or preferential rate.

This is why the tax code is not merely a revenue device.

It is also a spending device.

Some of the largest tax expenditures are not obscure loopholes. They are central features of American life.

The exclusion of employer-provided health insurance. Retirement saving preferences. Preferential rates on capital gains and dividends. The earned income tax credit. Housing-related deductions and exclusions.

Treasury estimates cited in the report place the exclusion for employer-provided health insurance at roughly $296 billion for fiscal year 2026. Retirement saving exclusions are on the order of $156 billion. Lower rates on capital gains and dividends are about $135 billion.

These are enormous numbers.

And they change how we should think about the state.

A country can have a large welfare state that spends through direct programs.

Or it can have a large hidden welfare state that spends through the tax code.

The United States does both.

But tax expenditures are less visible than appropriations. They are harder to see in ordinary political debate. They often feel like natural features of the tax system rather than policy choices.

That is their power.

A direct spending program announces itself.

A tax preference hides inside the baseline.

## 9. Progressivity: the system is progressive, but unevenly

The federal tax system is progressive overall.

That means higher-income groups pay a larger share of their income in federal taxes than lower-income groups.

The report cites CBO distributional data for 2022 showing that the effective federal tax rate rose from about 1.4 percent for the bottom quintile to 23.2 percent for the top quintile and 31.5 percent for the top 1 percent.

That is a progressive pattern.

For the individual income tax alone, IRS-based published data for tax year 2023 show the top 1 percent earned 20.6 percent of adjusted gross income and paid 40.4 percent of federal individual income taxes.

But this does not mean every part of the system is progressive.

The income tax is progressive.

Payroll taxes are less progressive.

Excise taxes can be regressive.

Tariffs can burden consumers through prices.

Capital gains preferences disproportionately benefit people with capital gains.

State and local taxes can be less progressive than the federal system because they rely more heavily on sales and property taxes.

So the correct statement is not "the tax system is progressive" or "the tax system is regressive."

The correct statement is more architectural.

The federal system is progressive overall, but it is built out of instruments with different distributional profiles.

Some push upward.

Some push downward.

Some are neutral in form but not in effect.

The final burden is the sum of these layers.

## 10. Compliance: the gap between law and collection

A tax system is not only what the law says.

It is what the state can actually collect.

That brings us to the tax gap.

The IRS reports an average gross tax gap of $496 billion for tax years 2014 to 2016, and a projected average gross tax gap of $540 billion for 2017 to 2019. After late payments and enforcement, the projected net tax gap is $470 billion.

This is not a small administrative detail.

It is one of the largest fiscal facts in the entire system.

The tax gap is the difference between taxes owed and taxes paid on time.

Its largest component is underreporting.

That matters.

The main compliance problem is not only people refusing to pay known bills. It is income being misreported, understated, or not fully visible to the system.

This reveals another deep principle.

Taxes are easier to collect when the government has third-party information.

Wages are reported by employers. Financial institutions report interest. Brokerages report capital transactions. Platforms issue forms. Withholding happens automatically.

But where income is harder to observe, compliance becomes more difficult.

The tax system is therefore also an information system.

It depends on visibility.

The more visible the income, the easier the collection.

The less visible the income, the more enforcement matters.

This is why IRS funding, audits, reporting rules, and administrative capacity are not side issues. They are part of the architecture.

A tax law without enforcement capacity is partly aspirational.

## 11. Federal law changes: the layered code

The current federal tax system did not emerge from one design.

It is the result of stacked reforms.

The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act lowered the corporate rate to 21 percent and changed the individual tax system, with many provisions originally set to expire.

The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 added the corporate alternative minimum tax, the stock buyback excise tax, and major IRS funding.

Later legislation reduced part of that IRS funding.

Then the 2025 One, Big, Beautiful Bill Act significantly changed federal taxes, credits, and deductions, and made the 2017-style seven-rate individual schedule effectively permanent.

Then 2026 brought major tariff changes.

This is how tax systems usually evolve.

Not by clean replacement.

By sediment.

One law creates a structure. Another law modifies it. A third law extends it. A fourth law rescinds part of the funding. A fifth law adds new credits. A sixth law changes the trade layer.

The result is not a simple machine.

It is a machine with history inside it.

Every bracket, exclusion, credit, phaseout, and deduction has a political biography.

## 12. State and local taxes: the second tax universe

Now leave the federal system.

State and local taxation is a different universe.

At the state and local level, property taxes matter enormously. Sales taxes matter more. Income taxes vary widely by state. Local governments become central actors.

Using the BEA framework for Q4 2025, state and local current tax receipts were annualized at about $2.725 trillion.

The major categories were property taxes at about $805 billion, income taxes at about $793 billion, sales taxes at about $633 billion, and excise taxes around $294 billion.

This is a very different structure from the federal system.

At the federal level, individual income and payroll taxes dominate.

At the state-local level, property, income, and sales taxes form a more balanced triangle.

This changes the politics.

Property taxes are tied to land, housing, schools, local services, assessments, and municipal finance.

Sales taxes are tied to consumption.

State income taxes are tied to wages, business income, capital gains, and residency.

Local taxes often feel more concrete because they are tied to schools, roads, police, fire departments, counties, cities, and special districts.

The federal tax system funds the national state.

The state-local tax system funds the everyday state.

The state-local system is also more geographically fragmented.

Some states have no broad-based individual income tax. Some rely heavily on income taxes. Some rely heavily on sales taxes. Some allow significant local add-ons. Some have property-tax limits. Some reassess frequently. Others constrain assessments.

There is no single state model.

America is not just one tax system.

It is a federation of tax systems.

## 13. California: the volatile high-income model

California is one of the most important examples because its structure is unusual.

California relies heavily on the personal income tax.

The report notes that California's personal income tax is estimated to account for nearly 60 percent of General Fund revenue before transfers in 2024-25. The sales tax accounts for about 15 percent.

That is a major design choice.

California has a highly progressive personal income tax, with rates reaching 13.3 percent at the top end. It also has an 8.84 percent corporation tax rate, a 10.84 percent rate for banks and financials, and a 6.65 percent corporate alternative minimum tax.

But the key feature is not just progressivity.

It is volatility.

California's revenue system is highly exposed to high-income taxpayers, capital gains, equity markets, and technology-sector compensation.

When markets rise, California can collect enormous revenue.

When markets fall, revenue can weaken sharply.

This is the tradeoff.

A highly progressive income tax can be productive. It can raise large amounts of money from high-income taxpayers.

But if the tax base is concentrated in volatile income, the budget becomes volatile too.

California also has a distinctive property-tax regime because of Proposition 13.

Proposition 13 limited the general property-tax rate to 1 percent of assessed value, plus voter-approved debt rates, and restricted annual assessed-value growth until reassessment events.

This means California does not operate like a state that constantly reassesses property at full market value.

So California combines a highly progressive and volatile income-tax system with a constitutionally constrained property-tax system.

That is not an accident. It is the state's fiscal constitution.

## 14. Federal-state interaction: the SALT cap and PTET

One of the most revealing features of American taxation is that state tax systems respond to federal tax law.

The report gives an important example: California's pass-through entity elective tax, or PTET.

Because federal law capped the state and local tax deduction, states looked for workarounds, especially for pass-through business owners.

California's PTET is one of these mechanisms.

The 2026-27 Governor's Budget says that because federal H.R. 1, Public Law 119-21, permanently extended the federal SALT cap, California's earlier statutory trigger extends the state PTET from 2026 through 2030.

That extension is projected to increase corporation tax revenue by $22.9 billion and reduce personal income-tax revenue by $20.2 billion over the budget window, with a small positive net timing effect.

This is technical, but the principle is simple.

Federal tax law changes state behavior.

State tax law then changes taxpayer behavior.

And taxpayer behavior changes revenue.

The American tax system is not a hierarchy of isolated layers.

It is an interactive system.

Federal rules reshape state rules.

State rules reshape taxpayer planning.

Taxpayer planning reshapes revenue.

Revenue reshapes budgets.

Budgets reshape politics.

That is the machine.

## 15. What the tax system reveals

Now step back.

The tax system is often discussed as if it were mainly about fairness.

Who pays too much? Who pays too little? Should rates be higher? Should rates be lower?

Those questions matter.

But they are not enough.

A tax system reveals several deeper things.

First, it reveals state capacity.

Can the government see the income? Can it measure the transaction? Can it enforce the rule? Can it collect automatically?

Second, it reveals political compromise.

Every deduction, exclusion, and credit is a settlement between competing interests. Some are broad and popular. Some are narrow and technical. Some are economically defensible. Some survive because removing them would create political pain.

Third, it reveals economic priorities.

The code subsidizes employer health insurance. It subsidizes retirement saving. It favors some capital income. It supports low-income workers through credits. It encourages certain investments. It penalizes certain products. It taxes some imports.

Fourth, it reveals federalism.

The federal government, states, and localities do not merely divide responsibilities. They divide tax instruments. The federal government dominates income and payroll. Local governments lean on property. States choose mixtures of income, sales, corporate, excise, and other taxes.

Fifth, it reveals visibility.

The easiest income to tax is income the system can already see.

This is why wages are such a powerful tax base. They are measured, reported, and withheld.

The harder something is to see, the more the system depends on audits, reporting rules, and enforcement.

A tax code is therefore not only a law book.

It is a surveillance map of economic life.

## 16. The final model

So here is the simplest model of American taxation.

At the federal level, the government funds itself mainly through individual income taxes and payroll taxes.

The income tax is progressive, bracketed, and full of definitions, deductions, credits, and preferences.

The payroll tax is automatic, wage-centered, and tied to social insurance.

Corporate taxes matter, but they are much smaller than individual income and payroll taxes.

Capital income receives distinct treatment.

Estate taxes are small in revenue but large in symbolism.

Excise taxes and tariffs operate through prices and products rather than ordinary income.

Tax expenditures turn the code into a spending machine.

Compliance depends on visibility, reporting, withholding, and enforcement.

At the state and local level, the structure changes.

Property taxes, sales taxes, and state income taxes become central. Local governments matter. Geography matters. State design matters.

And in California, the system becomes a high-revenue, high-volatility model built around progressive income taxation, constrained property taxation, and sensitivity to capital gains.

That is the architecture.

Not a single tax system.

A stack of fiscal machines.

Some visible. Some hidden. Some progressive. Some regressive. Some automatic. Some volatile. Some designed. Some inherited.

And all of them together form the cash constitution of the United States.

## Closing

Taxes are usually presented as a burden.

That is true, but incomplete.

They are also a map.

A map of what the state can reach.

A map of what society chooses to subsidize.

A map of which incomes are visible and which are hard to see.

A map of federalism, class structure, property, labor, capital, consumption, inheritance, and political compromise.

To understand American taxes is not just to understand a bill.

It is to understand one of the central operating systems of the country.
