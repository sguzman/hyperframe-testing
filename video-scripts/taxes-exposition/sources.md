Here is the resource pack I would use for the U.S. taxes video. I would treat this like a **source bench**: government data for authority, Tax Foundation for clear explanatory charts, and a few think tanks for distributional and policy framing.

## 1. Core data sources

### Congressional Budget Office: federal revenue breakdown

Use this for the cleanest macro-level chart: **where federal tax revenue comes from**.

The CBO’s FY2025 revenue infographic says federal revenues totaled about **$5.2 trillion**, with more than half from individual income taxes. ([Congressional Budget Office][1])

Visuals to make:

| Visual                                 | Use                                                                                    |
| -------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Pie chart: Federal revenue by source   | Individual income tax, payroll tax, corporate tax, excise, customs, estate/gift, other |
| Stacked bar: Federal revenue over time | Shows how income/payroll taxes dominate the modern system                              |
| Simple hierarchy chart                 | “Federal government collects mainly from income and payroll”                           |

Good source:
CBO - *Revenues in Fiscal Year 2025: An Infographic* ([Congressional Budget Office][1])

---

### U.S. Treasury Fiscal Data: receipts by source

Use this when you want exact numbers. Treasury’s FY2025 combined statement gives federal receipt categories: **individual income taxes $2.656T**, **social insurance and retirement receipts $1.748T**, **corporate income taxes $452.1B**, **excise taxes $105.9B**, and other categories including estate/gift and customs. ([Bureau of the Fiscal Service][2])

Visuals to make:

| Visual               | Use                                                            |
| -------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Horizontal bar chart | “The federal tax machine, ranked by size”                      |
| Animated build       | Add each revenue source one by one                             |
| Ratio graphic        | “Income tax + payroll tax = the core of the federal tax state” |

Good source:
Treasury - *Receipts by Source, FY2025* ([Bureau of the Fiscal Service][2])

---

### IRS: current tax brackets and inflation adjustments

Use this for explaining **marginal tax rates**, **standard deduction**, and why people misunderstand “being in a bracket.” The IRS says that for tax year 2026 the top rate remains **37%**, applying to single filers above **$640,600** and married joint filers above **$768,700**. ([IRS][3])

Visuals to make:

| Visual                        | Use                                                                |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Stair-step marginal tax chart | Explains brackets visually                                         |
| Taxable income pipeline       | Gross income → adjustments → deductions → taxable income → credits |
| “Bucket” animation            | Each slice of income taxed at its own rate                         |

Good source:
IRS - *Tax inflation adjustments for tax year 2026* ([IRS][3])

## 2. Tax Foundation sources

Tax Foundation is ideal for **clean explainers**, **comparative state maps**, and **tax type summaries**. Use it heavily for visuals, but anchor controversial claims with CBO, IRS, Treasury, or Census when possible.

### Federal, state, and local revenue by tax type

Tax Foundation reports that in 2023, individual income taxes were the largest U.S. tax revenue source across federal, state, and local governments, at **39.9% of total tax revenue**. ([Tax Foundation][4])

Good source:
Tax Foundation - *Sources of U.S. Tax Revenue by Tax Type* ([Tax Foundation][4])

## 3. Distributional sources: who pays?

### CBO: distribution of household income and federal taxes

Use this for the question: **who actually bears the federal tax burden?** CBO’s distribution work tracks household income before and after transfers and federal taxes. Its 2022 report says the top 1% share of income before transfers and taxes increased from **9% in 1979** to **18% in 2022**. ([Congressional Budget Office][7])

Visuals to make:

| Visual                                     | Use                             |
| ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------- |
| Effective federal tax rate by income group | Shows progressivity             |
| Before/after tax income distribution       | Shows tax-and-transfer effect   |
| Top 1% income composition                  | Labor income vs. capital income |

Good sources:
CBO - *The Distribution of Household Income, 2022* ([Congressional Budget Office][7])
CBO - *Distribution of Federal Taxes* topic page ([Congressional Budget Office][8])

---

### Tax Policy Center: federal revenue explanation

Useful for simplified language. TPC summarizes that over half of federal revenue comes from individual income taxes, around 30% from payroll taxes, and around 9% from corporate income taxes. ([Tax Policy Center][9])

Visuals to make:

| Visual                         | Use                                    |
| ------------------------------ | -------------------------------------- |
| Simplified federal revenue pie | Good for audience-friendly pacing      |
| “Three pillars” graphic        | Income tax, payroll tax, corporate tax |

Good source:
Tax Policy Center - *What are the sources of revenue for the federal government?* ([Tax Policy Center][9])

## 4. Tax gap and enforcement sources

### IRS: tax gap

Use this for the enforcement section: taxes are not just rules; they are a compliance system.

The IRS estimates the **gross tax gap** for tax year 2022 at **$696 billion**, with a voluntary compliance rate of **85.0%**. ([IRS][10])

Visuals to make:

| Visual                           | Use                                                                 |
| -------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| “Owed vs. paid” gap graphic      | Explains tax gap                                                    |
| Sankey chart                     | Total true tax → paid voluntarily → late/enforced payments → unpaid |
| Bar chart: components of tax gap | Underreporting, underpayment, nonfiling                             |

Good sources:
IRS - *The Tax Gap* ([IRS][10])
IRS - *Tax Gap Projections for Tax Year 2022* ([IRS][11])

## 5. Tax expenditures: hidden spending through the tax code

### Treasury: tax expenditure report

This is a killer section for a systems video. Tax expenditures are basically spending-like benefits routed through the tax code.

Treasury’s FY2027 tax expenditure report lists the exclusion of employer contributions for medical insurance premiums and medical care as one of the largest tax expenditures, projected at **$279.18B in 2026** and rising in later years. ([U.S. Department of the Treasury][12])

Visuals to make:

| Visual                         | Use                                                                                        |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| “Shadow budget” graphic        | Spending through tax preferences                                                           |
| Top tax expenditures bar chart | Health insurance exclusion, retirement preferences, capital gains, mortgage interest, etc. |
| Split screen                   | Direct spending vs. tax expenditure                                                        |

Good source:
Treasury - *Tax Expenditures, Fiscal Year 2027* ([U.S. Department of the Treasury][12])

## 6. State/local tax system sources

### BEA: government receipts and expenditures

BEA is useful for macroeconomic context and broad government revenue aggregates. It reports government receipts and expenditures quarterly; for Q4 2025, current receipts were listed at **$8.9517T** annualized and current expenditures at **$10.8313T** annualized. ([Bureau of Economic Analysis][13])

Visuals to make:

| Visual                           | Use                               |
| -------------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| Federal + state + local receipts | Big-picture government finance    |
| Receipts vs. expenditures        | Shows fiscal imbalance            |
| Timeline chart                   | Tax receipts over business cycles |

Good source:
BEA - *Government Receipts and Expenditures* ([Bureau of Economic Analysis][13])

---

### California Legislative Analyst’s Office: state tax case study

Use California as a case study because it clearly shows how a state can rely heavily on income tax, sales tax, corporate tax, and property tax.

LAO’s *CalFacts 2024* says California state and local governments raise around **$300 billion per year from taxes**, mostly from personal income, corporate profits, retail sales, and property values. ([Legislative Analyst's Office][14])

Visuals to make:

| Visual                   | Use                                                      |
| ------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| California tax stack     | Shows one state’s revenue model                          |
| Property tax explainer   | Proposition 13 as institutional constraint               |
| “Volatile revenue” chart | Income-tax-heavy states rise and fall with capital gains |

Good sources:
LAO - *CalFacts 2024* ([Legislative Analyst's Office][14])
LAO - *Understanding California’s Property Taxes* ([Legislative Analyst's Office][15])

### Pie chart 2: Total U.S. tax revenue by tax type

Use Tax Foundation for federal/state/local combined tax structure. This chart should show that the full U.S. system is not just federal income tax; it is income, payroll, property, sales, corporate, excise, and other taxes layered together. ([Tax Foundation][4])

---

### Pie chart 3: State and local tax mix

Use Tax Foundation’s state/local collections data or Census/BEA if you want the raw official data. This is where you show that local governments often rely heavily on **property taxes**, while states often rely on **income taxes** and **sales taxes**. ([Tax Foundation][5])

---

### Pie chart 4: The taxpayer’s burden stack

This one is conceptual, not purely statistical. Use it to show a household facing:

| Layer              | Examples                         |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------- |
| Federal income tax | Form 1040, brackets, credits     |
| Payroll tax        | Social Security, Medicare        |
| State income tax   | Varies by state                  |
| Sales tax          | Consumption                      |
| Property tax       | Directly or through rent         |
| Excise taxes       | Gasoline, tobacco, alcohol, etc. |
| Tariffs/customs    | Embedded in prices               |

This is a good “systems” visual: taxes are not one tax. They are a layered interface between the person and multiple levels of government.

## 8. Strong visual metaphors for the video

### The tax system as a stack

Use layers:

1. Constitution
2. Congress
3. Internal Revenue Code
4. Treasury regulations
5. IRS forms and enforcement
6. Taxpayer behavior
7. Revenue collected
8. Spending and redistribution

This fits your premium systems style.

---

### The tax system as plumbing

Money flows from:

**Households / businesses → withholding / estimated payments / filing → IRS / state agencies / local governments → Treasury / budgets / programs**

Use animated pipes with valves:

| Valve       | Meaning                |
| ----------- | ---------------------- |
| Deductions  | Reduce taxable income  |
| Credits     | Reduce tax owed        |
| Withholding | Prepayment             |
| Refund      | Overpayment returned   |
| Audit       | Enforcement correction |
| Tax gap     | Leakage                |

---

### The tax code as a machine

Inputs:

| Input           | Tax treatment              |
| --------------- | -------------------------- |
| Wages           | Income tax + payroll tax   |
| Business profit | Income tax / corporate tax |
| Capital gains   | Preferential rules         |
| Property        | Local property tax         |
| Consumption     | Sales/excise taxes         |
| Imports         | Customs duties             |

Output:

**Revenue, incentives, redistribution, compliance burden, and political conflict.**

## 10. My recommended source hierarchy

For a polished, trustworthy video, I would use this hierarchy:

1. **CBO / Treasury / IRS** for hard claims.
2. **Tax Foundation** for maps, comparative state charts, and clean tax-policy framing.
3. **Tax Policy Center / CBPP / Cato / PGPF** for competing interpretive perspectives.
4. **State sources like California LAO** for concrete state-level examples.
5. **Your own custom charts** generated from the above, so the video looks original rather than like a slideshow of borrowed visuals.

The strongest visual spine is:

**Federal revenue pie → individual tax bracket staircase → payroll tax layer → state/local map → tax expenditures shadow budget → tax gap leakage → final systems diagram.**
